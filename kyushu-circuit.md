# Trip B: Fire, Food, and the Hidden Islands
### The Kyushu Circuit — 12 days

![Steam rising from a private outdoor onsen bath in a forested valley](https://picsum.photos/seed/kurokawa-onsen-steam/1400/560)
*Replace with a Kurokawa Onsen photo — search ["kurokawa onsen rotenburo"](https://unsplash.com/s/photos/japanese-onsen-outdoor) on Unsplash*

Three nights into this trip you are sitting in a private cedar-and-stone outdoor bath in a Kurokawa valley, steam rising from the water into the cold mountain air, surrounded by forest. The next morning you drive to the edge of one of the world's largest active calderas. A week later you are eating hand-stretched noodles boiled at your table in a remote Pacific island fishing village while planning the morning's sea kayak route to a Gothic church on a cliff. Kyushu is the Japan that experienced travelers haven't done, and it delivers something genuinely different at every stop.

This is also the itinerary that most cleanly solves the tattoo issue: private outdoor baths at Kurokawa, beach and ocean activities on Goto, and you spend very little time in facilities with communal bathing restrictions.

---

## At a glance

- **12 days / 11 nights**
- Tokyo (2N) → Fukuoka (2N) → Kurokawa Onsen + Aso (2N) → Nagasaki (2N) → Goto Islands (3N)
- 1 domestic flight (Tokyo → Fukuoka), 1 car circuit, 1 jetfoil ferry (Nagasaki → Goto)
- **Best timing: Early-to-mid June** (June 1–20). This is Kyushu's rainy season, but the Kurokawa and Goto sections are rain-compatible — onsen in rain is wonderful, and the Goto sea conditions tend to be better in early June before typhoon swell season. If you must go late June, conditions are similar; it's slightly warmer and some days are clearer as tsuyu ends.
- This is the most weather-flexible itinerary of the three — rain enhances Kurokawa and doesn't significantly affect Goto, Nagasaki, or Fukuoka.

---

## What makes this trip compete with Turkey or Greece

The Goto Islands are the answer. They are a chain of subtropical Pacific islands with a deeply strange and beautiful history: hidden Christian communities that practiced their faith in secret for 250 years after Christianity was banned, building churches in remote fishing villages using techniques learned from Dutch traders, eventually emerging in the 1870s to construct Gothic and Romanesque brick churches that still hold active Sunday services. The islands have excellent snorkeling visibility (15-25 meters, better than most of the Mediterranean), resident bottlenose dolphin pods, handmade noodles unlike anything else in Japan, and almost no international tourists. Compare this to an afternoon in Cinque Terre.

Add Fukuoka — which many Japanese food obsessives consider the country's best eating city — and Kurokawa, which looks like a fairytale onsen village in a mountain valley, and you have a trip that's genuinely hard to replicate.

## What each person gets

**Christian** stands at the rim of the Aso caldera at sunrise — one of the world's largest calderas, 25 kilometers across, with an active smoking volcanic cone at its center. Even if the crater itself is restricted that day, the landscape from the rim is unlike anything else: a volcanic moonscape inside an ancient ring of mountains, with a turquoise sulfurous lake visible below. Nagasaki at night from Mt. Inasa is a perfect city panorama. The Kashiragashima Church on its dramatic promontory over the Pacific Ocean is one of the most photogenic single subjects in Japan.

**Eliza** sits in a private outdoor cedar bath at Kurokawa Onsen while dinner is being prepared a few meters away. Kurokawa is the version of Japanese onsen that actually looks like the pictures — a hidden valley town of steam and timber-framed ryokan, with private baths that can be reserved so there are no tattoo complications. The kaiseki dinner at a Kurokawa ryokan — 8-10 courses of Kumamoto ingredients (local river fish, venison, mountain vegetables) in delicate Japanese form — is one of the best meals on any of these three trips. Fukuoka's shopping is outstanding (Canal City, Tenjin underground, the Daimyo indie boutique neighborhood). Nagasaki is charming.

**Phil** hikes the Kuju mountain range — a 4-5 hour alpine traverse from a 1,330m trailhead across a volcanic plateau to Kujusan (1,787m), one of the highest peaks in Kyushu. This is a serious mountain day with no crowds, beautiful wildflowers in early June, and the kind of silence that comes from being somewhere almost no international tourists go. On the Goto Islands, he sea kayaks along dramatic coastline to remote church headlands, dives water with 20-meter visibility, and potentially encounters the resident dolphin pod from the water rather than a boat.

**Lillian**, if she's pregnant, does this trip without compromise. She cannot use the communal onsen at Kurokawa — but she can: eat the kaiseki dinner, walk the beautiful onsen-town lanes in the morning mist, and use a kashikiri private bath (book a room with an attached outdoor bath, confirm lower temperature is available). At Aso she drives through the Kusasenri highland meadow — a broad alpine plateau inside the caldera with wild horses grazing against the volcanic backdrop — which is as dramatic as any hike and requires nothing more than a short walk from the parking area. Nagasaki is a city made for walking at a relaxed pace. On Goto she cycles island roads, takes the dolphin tour boat, visits the churches, and swims at uncrowded beaches.

**Steph** books the Goto dolphin tour. Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins are resident in the island waters year-round, and operators report encounter rates of around 90% in calm conditions. She encounters them from a small boat, then later watches for them from a kayak. She thrift-shops Fukuoka's Daimyo neighborhood — a tight cluster of independent boutiques and vintage shops that has developed into one of Kyushu's best shopping circuits. At Aso, the highland grassland has wild boar and deer visible at dawn if you know where to look. And as the group's vegetarian: Nagasaki has a strong shojin ryori (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine) tradition, and Fukuoka's food stalls include excellent vegetable-focused options. Goto's seafood-focused food culture is more challenging for a strict vegetarian, but the Goto udon is vegetarian-compatible and exceptional.

**Juan** eats through Fukuoka, which is an extraordinary food city: yatai (outdoor food stalls) on the riverbanks of Nakagawa with grilled skewers and cold Sapporo under bare-bulb lighting, tonkotsu ramen at a counter at 11pm, mentaiko (Hakata's spicy cod roe) on everything, fresh tuna at Nagahama fish market before the city wakes up. Then in Nagasaki: chanpon (a seafood noodle soup that arrived via Chinese traders and became a Nagasaki institution), castella (Portuguese sponge cake, here for 400 years, available in every style), sara udon, and a food culture that doesn't resemble Tokyo's or Osaka's at all. On Goto, he eats jigoku-daki ("hell-boiling") udon — hand-stretched with camellia oil, boiled at the table, dipped in flying-fish broth with raw egg. This is genuinely unlike anything else he will have eaten. Fukuoka's coffee scene is also very good — independent third-wave roasters in the backstreet areas of Yakuin and Daimyo.

## Getting there and back

**SFO → Tokyo:** Nonstop on ANA, JAL, or United (~11 hrs) into Haneda.

**Tokyo → Fukuoka:** Domestic flight, Haneda to Fukuoka (FUK), ~1.5 hours. Multiple daily flights on ANA and JAL.

**Fukuoka → Kurokawa → Nagasaki:** Rent a car in Fukuoka and drive the circuit.
- Fukuoka to Kurokawa: ~1.5 hours by expressway
- Kurokawa to Aso (day trip): ~45-60 min drive
- Kurokawa to Nagasaki: ~1.5-2 hours by expressway via Kumamoto
- Return car at Nagasaki; pick up separate cars on Goto Island for the island section

**Nagasaki → Goto Islands (Fukue):** Jetfoil (Kyushu Shosen) from Nagasaki Port to Fukue, ~1 hour 25 minutes. About 3-4 departures per day. **Book in advance for 6 people** — the jetfoil has limited seats.

**Departure from Japan:** Fly from Nagasaki Airport (NGS) → Haneda (~1h40m) → SFO nonstop. Or take the Kamome limited express from Nagasaki to Hakata/Fukuoka (~2h) and fly from Fukuoka. Either works; Nagasaki Airport is convenient if you're starting the departure from there.

## Section by section

### Tokyo (2 nights)

Short and focused. The group has been here. Use these two days for jet lag recovery and one or two specific activities, not a tour of the highlights.

Suggestions:
- **Juan:** Kappabashi kitchen street and Tsukiji outer market morning.
- **Steph:** Shimokitazawa or Koenji for vintage.
- **Eliza:** Omotesando or Ginza for a few hours.
- One strong group dinner, booked ahead.

### Fukuoka (2 nights)

Fukuoka is a compact, extremely walkable city with a justified reputation as Japan's best food destination. It's also the only major Japanese city that routinely allows outdoor food stalls (yatai) — a dozen or more wooden tent-stalls are set up nightly along the riverbanks of the Nakagawa and around Tenjin, each serving grilled skewers, ramen, and cold beer to 8-10 people at a counter.

**Food is the activity in Fukuoka. Plan accordingly:**
- **Yatai stalls** (evening, Nakagawa area) — find a stall that has space for 6, order everything on the menu, drink cold beer while the city walks past. Do this on your first night to set the tone.
- **Hakata tonkotsu ramen** — the original creamy pork-bone broth. Ichiran (private booth ramen, a meditative solo experience) or Shin-Shin (lighter version). Do both if time allows.
- **Mentaiko** — Hakata's spicy cod roe, used here as a condiment, seasoning, and pasta sauce. Available everywhere.
- **Nagahama fish market** (early morning, near the port) — tuna auction viewing is possible with advance registration, or just browse the market stalls and find a breakfast stall with fresh sashimi.
- **Mizutaki** — Fukuoka's famous chicken hot pot, with collagen-rich broth from long-simmered whole chicken. A proper dinner option for the group.

**Beyond food:**
- **Canal City** — a genuinely interesting indoor-outdoor mall with a canal running through it. Eliza's domain. Also has the best ramen theme floor (Ramen Stadium, top floor).
- **Daimyo neighborhood** — indie boutiques, vintage stores, good coffee shops, small bars. Steph's domain.
- **Ohori Park** — a beautiful lake park with good jogging path (5km loop) for morning runs. Phil and Christian.
- **Fukuoka Castle ruins / Maizuru Park** — pleasant morning walk.

**Pick up the rental car in Fukuoka** (from the airport or city center) on Day 4 morning. You'll drive it all the way to Nagasaki.

### Kurokawa Onsen + Aso (2 nights)

![Mt. Aso volcanic caldera — one of the world's largest active calderas](https://picsum.photos/seed/aso-volcano-caldera/1400/500)
*Replace with a Mt. Aso photo — search ["mount aso volcano crater japan"](https://unsplash.com/s/photos/mount-aso-volcano) on Unsplash*

Drive from Fukuoka to Kurokawa: ~1.5 hours south on the expressway into the mountains of Kumamoto Prefecture. As you drop into the valley, the ryokan appear through steam and cedar. Check in. Don't plan anything else for arrival day.

**Kurokawa Onsen** is a small mountain town — more of a cluster of ryokan than a town — built along the Tanoharu River in a forested gorge. Its reputation is based entirely on its onsen: each ryokan has its own outdoor baths, and the famous "nyuto tegata" passport system lets guests wander between properties' baths. The atmosphere is genuinely what ryokan brochures promise but rarely deliver.

**The tattoo situation:** Private kashikiri (reserved) outdoor baths are available at most Kurokawa ryokan and are the solution for Juan and Steph. A private kashikiri is booked by the hour and is exclusively yours — tattoo policy is effectively irrelevant. Many ryokan also offer room types with a private attached rotenburo (outdoor bath on your deck), which is even better. **When booking, specify:** (a) you need 3 rooms, (b) two guests have tattoos and you need kashikiri access or in-room outdoor bath options, and confirm in writing. Ask explicitly.

**Lillian if pregnant:** Onsen are generally contraindicated in pregnancy due to hyperthermia risk. She should not use the communal baths. A brief soak at a lower temperature in a kashikiri may be acceptable depending on her doctor's advice — confirm before the trip. In any case, she can: walk the beautiful onsen-town lanes in the morning, eat the kaiseki dinner (the meal is the heart of the Kurokawa stay), and enjoy the ryokan atmosphere. This is a genuinely pleasant stop even without getting in the water.

**Recommended properties:** Sanga (forest setting along the Tanoharu River, excellent kaiseki, kashikiri baths available), Yamamizuki (one of Kurokawa's most prestigious, in-room outdoor baths in some room types), Yamabiko (atmospheric, mid-range by Kurokawa standards). Book as early as possible for June — Kurokawa fills up 2-3 months ahead for weekend nights.

**Day trip to Aso caldera (Day 6):** Drive 45-60 min to the Aso area.

**What Mt. Aso actually is:** The Aso caldera is one of the world's largest, roughly 25km across, formed by an ancient volcanic eruption so massive it collapsed an entire mountain. What you're driving through on the approach is the floor of a prehistoric supervolcano — farmland, small towns, and roads are all inside the caldera. In the center rises a ring of five volcanic peaks, with Nakadake as the active cone.

- **Kusasenri plateau** — a broad green meadow inside the caldera with a shallow lake and wild horses grazing. The scale of the volcanic landscape behind them is staggering. Easy 20-minute walk from the parking area. Excellent for everyone including Lillian.
- **Daikanbo lookout** — arguably the best single panoramic view of the entire caldera from the rim. A 15-minute walk from the viewpoint parking.
- **Nakadake crater:** If the Japan Meteorological Agency alert level permits access (check current level ~2 weeks before departure), you can drive or hike to the crater rim and walk along the edge of an active smoking volcanic crater over a vivid turquoise acidic lake. When open, this is extraordinary. **But treat this as a bonus, not a certainty** — the crater has been at Level 2 (restricted) or higher for extended periods in recent years, and you cannot reliably count on it.
- **If the crater is closed or Phil wants a serious hike:** The **Kuju Mountain Range** (30-45 min drive north of Kurokawa) is the alternative and is arguably a better hiking experience. From the Makihatayama trailhead at 1,330m elevation, the round trip to Kujusan (1,787m, Kyushu's highest) takes 4-5 hours across an alpine volcanic plateau. Early June means wildflowers, some lingering snow on high traverses, and almost no crowds. Phil does this; others do the Aso scenic drive and Kusasenri. It's a natural split day.

Return to Kurokawa for the second ryokan kaiseki dinner. Don't skip it.

### Nagasaki (2 nights)

Drive from Kurokawa to Nagasaki: ~1.5-2 hours by expressway through Kumamoto.

Nagasaki is one of Japan's most historically charged and visually distinctive cities. For 250 years, it was the only port open to outside trade — the Dutch East India Company, Chinese merchants, and later Portuguese traders gave the city a hybrid culture, cuisine, and architecture that is genuinely different from anywhere else in Japan. The atomic bomb of August 1945 adds another layer. The city itself is hilly and built around a beautiful harbor, with streetcar lines running along the waterfront.

**What to do:**
- **Atomic Bomb Museum and Peace Park** — sobering, necessary, and extremely well done. Allow 2-3 hours. The museum is one of the best historical museums in Japan.
- **Glover Garden** — the hillside Victorian-era mansions built by Western merchants in the 1860s-1900s, with expansive harbor views. Thomas Glover's house is the best preserved. The garden is beautiful.
- **Dejima** — the reconstructed Dutch trading post island (connected to the mainland by now), where the Dutch were required to live during the exclusion period. Small but fascinating — the history of what Japan chose to know and not know about the outside world, filtered through this tiny island.
- **Chinatown (Shinchi)** — active and atmospheric, with chanpon and sara udon restaurants.
- **Mt. Inasa night view** — cable car or bus to the top for a panoramic harbor-and-city view at night. One of Japan's "top 3 night views." Worth doing on the first evening.
- **Hollander Slope** — cobblestone street of European-style houses, good for wandering.
- **Meganebashi bridge** — the double-arch stone bridge that reflects in the water to form "spectacles."

**Food:** Nagasaki has a uniquely distinct food culture:
- **Chanpon** — a deep, creamy seafood and vegetable noodle soup that arrived from Chinese immigrants and became something entirely its own. Ringer Hut is the famous chain; local spots in Chinatown are better.
- **Sara udon** — the same ingredients as chanpon but over crispy fried noodles instead of soup.
- **Castella (kasutera)** — Portuguese sponge cake that arrived in the 1500s and was perfected over centuries. Fukusaya is the historic shop. Buy several boxes to take home.
- **Shippoku** — Nagasaki's traditional multi-course cuisine, a hybrid of Japanese, Chinese, and Dutch cooking. The most distinctively local dining experience. Reserve a shippoku restaurant for one evening dinner.
- For Steph (vegetarian): Shippoku has vegetable-forward dishes; tofu variations are common. Chanpon can be made vegetarian. Better options here than on the Goto Islands.

**Nagasaki in rain:** The city actually works very well in rain — the museums, Glover Garden (partially covered), covered shopping arcades (Hamanomachi), and restaurant-dense neighborhoods are all navigable. This is part of why the Kyushu circuit is the most weather-resilient of the three trips.

### Goto Islands — Fukue (3 nights)

![Dozaki Church on the Goto Islands — Gothic brick church above the Pacific](https://picsum.photos/seed/goto-islands-church/1400/500)
*Replace with a Goto Islands photo — search ["goto islands church japan"](https://unsplash.com/s/photos/goto-islands-japan) on Unsplash*

Take the jetfoil from Nagasaki Port to Fukue Port: 1 hour 25 minutes across the East China Sea. As you approach, the green hills of Fukuejima rise from the water with a small bay and fishing port below. It looks like somewhere in the Azores or the outer Hebrides. That is not a coincidence — it is the edge of an island chain that for centuries was the most isolated and secretive corner of Japan.

**The story of the Goto Islands:** After the Tokugawa government banned Christianity in 1597, Christian communities fled from mainland Kyushu to these remote islands where surveillance was lighter. For 250 years, they practiced their faith in complete secrecy — hiding altars behind sliding screens, disguising the Virgin Mary as a Buddhist Kannon statue, passing oral tradition through generations with no priests and no books. When Christianity was legalized in 1873, they emerged from hiding to find that most of what they believed was still intact. They then built churches — Gothic, Romanesque, brick, stone — on the same islands where they had hidden. 143 churches remain. The UNESCO designation in 2018 recognized not just the buildings but the entire history.

Visiting the churches is not a religious experience for most people. It is a human one.

**Base on Fukuejima.** It's the largest island, where the jetfoil docks, with the most accommodation and the best cycling infrastructure.

**Rent 2 cars on Fukue.** The island has rental car shops near the port. Essential for reaching remote churches and coastal viewpoints. Book ahead — the fleet is small.

**What to do:**

- **Churches — the circuit:** Spend at least one day driving the church circuit on Fukue and a day-ferry to a neighboring island.
  - **Dozaki Church (Fukuejima):** The most visited, a twin-towered Gothic brick church completed in 1908, set on a headland above the sea. 20 min drive from the port.
  - **Mizunoura Church (Fukuejima):** A wooden church on a hillside. Quieter and more intimate.
  - **Kashiragashima Church (Nakadori Island):** The most dramatically sited — on a rocky promontory above the sea, surrounded on three sides by water. Reachable by ferry to Nakadori. One of the most photographed subjects in Kyushu.
  - Day trip to Nakadori or Naradojima is possible by inter-island ferry from Fukue.

- **Dolphin watching:** Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins are resident in the Goto Island waters year-round. Tours depart from Fukue port, run about 1-1.5 hours, encounter rates reportedly around 90% in calm conditions. Cost ~¥3,000-4,000 per person. **Book ahead for 6 people** — the boats are small. For Steph: this is the main event. For Phil: being in the water near them on a kayak the next day is even better.

- **Sea kayaking:** Local operators offer guided half-day sea kayak tours along Fukue's coast. The island has sea caves, dramatic rock formations, and coastal church viewpoints that are only accessible from the water. Book for the group in advance — operators are small and 6 people requires a larger booking than usual.

- **Snorkeling and diving:** The Goto Islands have some of Kyushu's best water clarity (15-25 meters in June). Dive shops in Fukue town offer guided dives. Phil: this is good diving — reef fish, clear water, excellent for him and potentially for others. Lillian (if pregnant): no scuba, but surface snorkeling is appropriate (check with doctor); the water is warm enough in late June (22-24°C) for a wetsuit-free snorkel.

- **Cycling:** Fukue's southern half makes a good 35-40km bike loop (rental e-bikes available in the port town — book ahead for 6) passing Dozaki Church, coastal viewpoints, and quiet fishing villages. Not mandatory but good for a half-day when others are kayaking.

**Goto udon:** The islands' most famous food. Hand-stretched, round-section noodles made with camellia oil, cooked differently from any other udon in Japan. Order the jigoku-daki ("hell-boiling") style: the noodles arrive in a communal pot of boiling water at your table, you fish them out at your preferred doneness, and dip them in a cold bowl of flying-fish (ago) broth with raw egg. The noodles are silky and firm simultaneously. It is genuinely unlike any udon you have eaten before. Every meal on the island should include some version of them.

**Accommodation:** The Goto Islands are not developed for luxury tourism — expect good, clean guesthouses and minshuku (family-run inns) rather than resort ryokan. For 3 couples, the best option is either booking 3 rooms at the same property (research directly with Fukue guesthouses; email the Goto City Tourism Office for current recommendations) or renting a house through Airbnb or Vacation Stay Japan that takes the whole group. A house rental is ideal — you stay together, there's a kitchen for one or two self-catered meals, and it's significantly cheaper than 3 ryokan rooms. Most guesthouse accommodation includes 2 meals (breakfast and dinner) — accept this, as restaurant options are limited especially in the evening.

**June weather on Goto:** Rainy season applies, but the island's location in the East China Sea means weather patterns differ from mainland Kyushu. Expect some rainy days but also clear stretches. Water visibility is excellent in June. Small boat tours (dolphin watching, kayaking) can be cancelled in rough conditions — the 3-night stay gives you some buffer.

## Key risks

**Aso crater access is unpredictable.** Do not plan the trip around the crater being open. Plan around Kusasenri meadow and caldera views (always accessible) and treat the crater as a bonus. The Kuju hike is planned and ready as the alternative, which is arguably a better hiking experience for Phil anyway.

**Goto small-boat tours can be cancelled** in rough weather. The 3-night Fukue stay gives a buffer — if Day 10's dolphin tour is cancelled, it can shift to Day 11. If both are rough, take it as an extra church/cycling/snorkeling day. June before the main typhoon season is generally good for sea conditions, but build flexibility.

**Kurokawa is genuinely in demand for June.** Weekend nights in particular book 2-3 months out. If weeknights are possible, they're easier to secure and cheaper. Confirm tattoo/private bath policy explicitly at booking.

**Lillian and onsen:** She genuinely cannot use hot spring baths in pregnancy. This means she misses part of what makes Kurokawa special. Consider: (a) book a room type with a private attached outdoor bath and she can soak briefly at lower temperature with doctor clearance, or (b) frame Kurokawa as a spectacular dinner and atmosphere stay where she happens not to be in the water. The kaiseki dinner and the ryokan setting are valuable without the bath.

**Vegetarian on Goto:** The islands are a seafood culture. If Steph is strictly vegetarian (no seafood), she'll need to navigate carefully on Goto. Goto udon itself is vegetarian-compatible (the broth contains flying fish; if that's acceptable, it's fine). Rice, vegetables, and tofu dishes exist. The group accommodation with a kitchen helps. This is a minor friction, not a trip-breaker.

**Departure logistics from the Goto Islands.** The return journey (Fukue → Nagasaki → Haneda → SFO or Fukue → Nagasaki Airport → Haneda → SFO) requires an early start on Day 12. Plan for it. Don't book a 6am jetfoil on the last day unless you have an early flight that genuinely requires it.
