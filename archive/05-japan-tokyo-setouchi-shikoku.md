# Option 5: Tokyo + Setouchi Islands + Shikoku

The art-and-cycling route. Three nights in Tokyo, three nights based in Takamatsu for day trips to Naoshima and Teshima (world-class contemporary art on tiny islands), two nights around the Shimanami Kaido for one of the world's great cycling routes, then two nights in Matsuyama for castle-town wandering and Japan's oldest hot spring. The most varied day-to-day experience of any option.

## At a glance

- 11 days / 10 nights
- Tokyo (3N) → Takamatsu (3N) → Onomichi/Shimanami Kaido (2N) → Matsuyama (2N)
- 4 travel days, 1 domestic flight (on departure only)
- Best for: art lovers, active travelers, couples who want variety without roughing it

## The honest case for this option

**Why pick it:** This itinerary has the widest range of daily experiences. You get Tokyo's urban energy, then a meditative day wandering through Tadao Ando museums on a quiet island, then an exhilarating cycling day across island-hopping bridges over the Inland Sea, then soaking in a 3,000-year-old hot spring in a castle town. No two days feel alike. The Setouchi art islands (Naoshima, Teshima) are genuinely world-class — the Chichu Art Museum and Teshima Art Museum are among the most memorable art experiences in Japan. The Shimanami Kaido is consistently ranked as one of the world's best cycling routes. And unlike the Hokuriku/Alps option, this route doesn't require buses on tricky schedules — it's mostly trains and ferries.

**Why not:** The shopping and nightlife are weaker than the Tokyo/Osaka corridor. Takamatsu and Matsuyama are pleasant mid-sized cities, not destinations for late-night dining or serious retail therapy. There's no beach-resort component (the Inland Sea is scenic but not tropical). And the art islands have a critical scheduling constraint: **most museums are closed on Mondays**, which means your itinerary needs to be built around the calendar. The Shimanami Kaido cycling day is also weather-dependent — heavy rain makes it unpleasant or unsafe.

## Getting there and back

**SFO → Tokyo:** Nonstop on United, ANA, JAL, Zipair. ~11 hours.

**Departure from Matsuyama:** Fly Matsuyama (MYJ) → Haneda (~80 min), then connect to a nonstop SFO flight. ANA and JAL both operate multiple daily Matsuyama-Haneda flights, and Jetstar flies to Narita. The alternative (train from Matsuyama to Okayama, shinkansen to Tokyo) takes 6+ hours — flying is much better for the end of the trip.

## How you move between sections

### Tokyo → Takamatsu (Day 4)

**Shinkansen to Okayama (~3h15m on Hikari/Kodama, or ~3h45m if using JR Pass which doesn't cover Nozomi), then JR Marine Liner to Takamatsu (~55 min).** Total: about 4-4.5 hours door to door.

This is the longest single transit day, but it's all smooth rail travel. Depart Tokyo mid-morning, arrive Takamatsu by early-to-mid afternoon. Head straight to a udon shop — Kagawa Prefecture is the udon capital of Japan and the noodles here are the best you'll ever eat.

### Takamatsu → Onomichi (Day 7)

JR Marine Liner back to Okayama (~55 min), then JR Sanyo Line to Onomichi (~1h20m). Total: about 2.5 hours with the transfer. Moderate and easy.

### Shimanami Kaido: Onomichi → Imabari (Day 8)

The cycling route IS the transit. You ride from Onomichi across six bridges and six islands to Imabari on the Shikoku side. (Details below.)

### Imabari → Matsuyama (Day 9)

JR Yosan Line limited express, about **35-40 minutes**. Very easy, frequent trains.

## Section by section

### Tokyo (3 nights)

Same landing zone as all options.

### Takamatsu + Art Islands (3 nights)

Your base for exploring the Setouchi Inland Sea art islands. Takamatsu is a pleasant mid-sized city on Shikoku's north coast, famous for udon and home to one of Japan's finest gardens.

**Takamatsu itself:**
- **Ritsurin Garden** — one of Japan's most beautiful gardens, arguably rivaling Kenrokuen. Sprawling, with ponds, bridges, and Mt. Shiun as a backdrop. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
- **Udon** — Kagawa Prefecture is called "Udon Prefecture" for a reason. Small, no-frills shops serve handmade udon for 200-500 yen that will ruin you for noodles anywhere else. Eat udon multiple times. Try: Udon Baka Ichidai (butter udon), Ueharaya, Yamashita Udon.
- **Takamatsu Port area** — the ferry terminal is in the city center, making island day trips easy.

**Naoshima (full day):**

A small island transformed into one of the world's most remarkable open-air art destinations by the Benesse Corporation and architect Tadao Ando.

- **Chichu Art Museum** — an underground museum built into a hillside. Houses Monet water lilies in a space designed so natural light plays across them, plus installations by Walter De Maria and James Turrell. One of the most extraordinary museum experiences anywhere. **Requires timed-entry tickets — book online as soon as the window opens (typically 1-2 months ahead).** For six people wanting the same time slot, book early.
- **Benesse House Museum** — art museum on a clifftop, also Tadao Ando. Open to day visitors.
- **Art House Project (Honmura village)** — artists have transformed old houses, a shrine, and other structures into installations scattered through a quiet fishing village. James Turrell's "Backside of the Moon" and Hiroshi Sugimoto's shrine are highlights. Plan 2-3 hours.
- **Yayoi Kusama pumpkins** — the iconic yellow pumpkin sculpture on the pier (reinstalled after being washed away in a 2021 typhoon) and the red pumpkin at Miyanoura port.
- **Lee Ufan Museum** and **Ando Museum** — smaller but worthwhile if time allows.
- **Getting there:** Ferry from Takamatsu to Miyanoura Port (~50-60 min), roughly 5-6 departures per day. First ferry ~8:00-8:15 AM, last return ~6:00-7:00 PM. Catch the first ferry to maximize your day.
- **Getting around the island:** Shuttle bus connects the three main areas (Miyanoura, Honmura, Benesse). Can get crowded.
- **Allow a full day minimum.** Ideally 1.5 days if you want to see everything without rushing.

**Teshima (full day or half day):**

- **Teshima Art Museum** (architect Ryue Nishizawa, artist Rei Naito) — a single shell-like concrete form open to the sky, where water droplets emerge from the floor. It is a meditative, one-of-a-kind experience. Many visitors rank it as the single most memorable art encounter in the Setouchi islands. **Worth the trip to the island for this alone.**
- Other sites: Les Archives du Coeur (Christian Boltanski), Teshima Yokoo House, Storm House.
- Ferry from Takamatsu: ~35 min (high-speed). Less frequent than Naoshima — roughly 3-5 per day.
- You can also island-hop directly between Naoshima and Teshima (~20-25 min ferry), allowing a combined two-island day if well-planned.

**Skip Shodoshima/Angel Road.** With three nights in the area, you don't have time to add a third island without rushing. Naoshima + Teshima are the priorities.

**CRITICAL: Monday closures.** Most Naoshima and Teshima museums are **closed on Mondays** (and sometimes Tuesdays after a Monday holiday). Plan your island days for Tuesday-Sunday. This may constrain which day of the week you depart Tokyo. See the sample schedule below.

**Where to stay:** Stay in Takamatsu and day-trip to the islands. Takamatsu has plenty of hotels for three couples and good restaurant options at night. Benesse House on Naoshima is the luxury option (40,000-80,000 yen/night per room, gives after-hours art access) but booking three rooms for the same dates is very difficult. Mainland is more practical.

### Onomichi + Shimanami Kaido (2 nights)

Onomichi is a charming hillside port town facing the Inland Sea, with temple walks, narrow lanes, and a cat-filled atmosphere. It's also the starting point for the Shimanami Kaido cycling route.

**Shimanami Kaido — the details:**

- **What it is:** A 70-75 km cycling route from Onomichi (Honshu) to Imabari (Shikoku), crossing six islands and six bridges over the Seto Inland Sea. Consistently ranked among the world's best cycling routes.
- **The experience:** Dedicated cycling lanes separate from highway traffic. Each bridge is a climb up a ramp (the bridges are high), then a descent on the other side. Between bridges, you ride through quiet island villages, past citrus groves and fishing ports, with the Inland Sea glittering on both sides.
- **Full route:** 70-75 km, 6-10 hours depending on fitness and stops. Total elevation gain ~500-600m (cumulative from bridge ramps). Most recreational cyclists do it in 7-8 hours with photo/lunch stops.
- **Is it realistic for a mixed-fitness group of six?** On regular bikes, the full route is a solid athletic day — hard for less fit riders. **E-bikes change everything.** On e-bikes, the full route is achievable for most reasonably active adults. Reserve e-bikes.
- **E-bike rental:** Available at the main terminal in Onomichi (near the ferry terminal — you take a 5-minute ferry to Mukaishima to start). One-way rental with drop-off in Imabari incurs a ~1,100 yen surcharge. E-bikes: ~2,500-3,000 yen/day. Regular bikes: ~1,100-2,000 yen/day. **Reserve in advance for six bikes.** Walk-up availability for six e-bikes is risky.
- **Battery range:** E-bike batteries generally last the full route, but heavy use of assist on hills can drain them. There are charging/swap stations at some terminals along the way.
- **Shorter/easier version:** Onomichi to Ikuchijima (~35-40 km, 3 bridges, the most scenic stretch). Stop at Setoda town on Ikuchijima, visit Kosanji Temple, return by bus. Half-day option: just the final Oshima-to-Imabari stretch (1 bridge, ~10 km).
- **Non-cycling alternative:** Regular buses cross the Shimanami Kaido highway from Onomichi/Fukuyama to Imabari in 1.5-2 hours. Non-cyclists can bus to Imabari and meet the riders there.
- **Luggage:** Ship bags from Onomichi to Matsuyama (or Imabari) via takkyubin (Yamato Transport). Essential — do not cycle with suitcases.

**Rain contingency:** With two nights in the Onomichi area, you have a buffer day. If it rains on your planned cycling day, postpone. If both days are rainy: light rain is doable but unpleasant. Heavy rain means take the bus — the bridges can be windy and slippery.

**Where to stay in Onomichi:** Smaller lodging market than Takamatsu. Look at guesthouses and small hotels in Onomichi proper, or hotels in Fukuyama (larger city, 20 min away by train). Alternative strategy: base in Imabari instead and cycle the reverse direction (less common but works).

### Matsuyama (2 nights)

A mid-sized castle town on Shikoku's northwest coast. The combination of Dogo Onsen (Japan's oldest hot spring) and one of Japan's best original castles makes this a satisfying final stop.

**What to do:**
- **Dogo Onsen** — one of Japan's oldest hot springs, the main bathhouse (Honkan) famously inspired the bathhouse in Spirited Away. It underwent extensive renovation starting in 2019 — by June 2026 it should be fully reopened (verify before your trip). The modern Dogo Onsen Annex (Asuka no Yu) has been fully operational since 2017 as a backup. Also: Tsubaki no Yu, a less famous but atmospheric public bath.
- **Matsuyama Castle** — one of only 12 original-construction castles in Japan. Sits on a hilltop reached by ropeway or chairlift. Excellent views over the city to the Inland Sea. The interior is original wood with samurai displays. 2-3 hours.
- **City wandering** — Matsuyama has a streetcar system, covered shopping arcades, and a relaxed pace. The Dogo Onsen neighborhood is pleasant for evening strolling.

**Two nights is the right amount.** Arrive from the Shimanami Kaido, soak in the onsen that evening. Next full day for the castle and city exploration, revisit the onsen in the evening. Depart the following morning.

## Day-by-day

*This schedule assumes a Saturday arrival to avoid Monday closures on the art islands.*

### Day 1 (Sat): Arrive in Tokyo
- Check in, easy neighborhood walk, casual dinner

### Day 2 (Sun): Tokyo — shared morning, split afternoon
- Coffee-and-walk morning together
- Shared lunch
- Afternoon split: shopping / active outing / free wandering
- One strong shared dinner

### Day 3 (Mon): Tokyo — flexible day
- Morning free
- Shimokitazawa shopping, gym, or photo walk
- Easy dinner

### Day 4 (Tue): Train to Takamatsu
- Shinkansen to Okayama (~3h15m), Marine Liner to Takamatsu (~55 min)
- Arrive mid-afternoon
- Walk to Ritsurin Garden (gorgeous even in late afternoon light)
- Udon dinner — your first of many

### Day 5 (Wed): Naoshima — full day
- First ferry from Takamatsu (~8:00 AM, arrive ~9:00 AM)
- Morning: Chichu Art Museum (with pre-booked timed tickets)
- Midday: Lee Ufan Museum, Yayoi Kusama pumpkins, Benesse House area
- Afternoon: Art House Project in Honmura village
- Last ferry back to Takamatsu
- Dinner in Takamatsu

### Day 6 (Thu): Teshima — full day (or split day)
- Ferry to Teshima (~35 min)
- Teshima Art Museum (arrive early for the quietest experience)
- Explore other Teshima sites: Les Archives du Coeur, Yokoo House
- Return to Takamatsu by mid-to-late afternoon
- Or: combine a Teshima morning with a quick return to Naoshima for anything you missed
- Final Takamatsu evening — udon, izakaya, wander the port area

### Day 7 (Fri): Train to Onomichi
- Morning free in Takamatsu
- Afternoon train to Onomichi via Okayama (~2.5 hrs total)
- Arrive late afternoon
- Walk Onomichi's hillside temple path and narrow lanes
- Ship luggage ahead to Matsuyama via takkyubin
- Easy dinner in town

### Day 8 (Sat): Shimanami Kaido cycling day
- Pick up e-bikes at the Onomichi terminal (reserve ahead)
- Short ferry to Mukaishima to start the route
- Cycle across the islands toward Imabari (full route: 70-75 km, or shorter version to Ikuchijima)
- Photo stops, lunch on one of the islands
- Non-cyclists: take the bus from Onomichi to Imabari and explore Imabari
- Arrive Imabari by late afternoon
- Train to Matsuyama (~35-40 min) if continuing same day, or stay in Imabari

### Day 9 (Sun): Arrive Matsuyama / Dogo Onsen evening
- If stayed in Imabari: morning train to Matsuyama (~35 min)
- If this is your rain buffer day for cycling: cycle today instead, arrive Matsuyama in the evening
- Check in, explore the Dogo Onsen neighborhood
- Evening soak at Dogo Onsen Honkan (or Asuka no Yu)
- Onsen-town dinner

### Day 10 (Mon): Matsuyama full day
- Morning: Matsuyama Castle — ropeway up, explore the original-construction keep, views over the city
- Lunch in the covered shopping arcade
- Afternoon: free time, city wandering by streetcar, revisit Dogo Onsen
- Final dinner together

### Day 11 (Tue): Fly home from Matsuyama
- Morning flight MYJ → Haneda (~80 min)
- Connect to nonstop SFO flight from HND or NRT
- Keep this day clean — no sightseeing before the airport

## Key risks

1. **Monday museum closures** on Naoshima and Teshima are a hard constraint. Plan your arrival and island days around the calendar. The sample schedule above avoids this, but any schedule shift needs to be checked against closures.
2. **Chichu Art Museum timed tickets** for six people at the same time — book as soon as the window opens.
3. **E-bike reservations for six** on the Shimanami Kaido — reserve well in advance. If you can't get e-bikes, the full route becomes much harder for mixed fitness.
4. **Rain on the cycling day** is a real possibility in June. The two-night Onomichi window gives you a buffer day, but if both days are wet, you'll need to bus across instead.
5. **June rainy season (tsuyu)** affects this region, though the Setouchi Inland Sea area is the driest part of Japan during tsuyu (mountains on both sides block moisture). Expect some rain but less than Tokyo, Osaka, or the Japan Sea coast.
6. **Luggage management** across four bases for six people — use takkyubin at every base change. Hotels will help you arrange it. Budget ~2,000-2,500 yen per bag per segment, ship a day ahead.
7. **Departure connection** from Matsuyama through Haneda — book early for six seats on the same domestic flight, and allow adequate connection time (3+ hours) for the international leg.

## Who should pick this

Choose this option if your group values variety over simplicity. This trip asks more of you logistically — timed art museum tickets, cycling reservations, Monday closures, luggage forwarding — but it rewards the effort with the widest range of daily experiences. If "Tadao Ando museum on a quiet island, then cycling across bridges over the Inland Sea, then soaking in Japan's oldest onsen" sounds like your group's idea of a great week, this is the one.
