# Trip E: Whales, Waterfalls, and the Mid-Atlantic
### Azores + Madeira — 12 days

![Sete Cidades twin crater lakes, Sao Miguel, Azores](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Lagoa_das_Sete_Cidades_%28Shutterstock%29.jpg/1280px-Lagoa_das_Sete_Cidades_%28Shutterstock%29.jpg)
*Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Lagoa_das_Sete_Cidades_(Shutterstock).jpg)*

On your second morning you are on a rigid inflatable boat in the open Atlantic, following radio instructions from a clifftop spotter who has been watching a pod of sperm whales surface for the last twenty minutes. The whales are fifty meters ahead. One rolls at the surface, exhales, and you see it clearly — a dark body longer than the boat. Then it arches, lifts its tail fluke, and dives straight down. Everyone on the boat is silent for five seconds. Nobody has seen anything like it. Three days later you are walking along a 500-year-old irrigation channel carved into a cliff face on Madeira, through a laurel forest that has existed since before humans arrived, with waterfalls crossing the path and the Atlantic 800 meters below. This trip is about encountering things at a scale that recalibrates your sense of what nature does.

---

## At a glance

- **12 days / 11 nights**
- Sao Miguel, Azores (4N) → travel day via Lisbon → Madeira (5N) → Porto Santo day trip → fly home
- 2 domestic flights (within Portugal), 1 inter-island ferry (Madeira ↔ Porto Santo)
- **Best timing: Early-to-mid June** (June 1–20). The Azores are driest in June–August, with June being the least crowded. Madeira is sunny and dry on the south coast in June. Whale watching is excellent — sperm whales are resident year-round, and late-spring migrating species (sei whales, fin whales) may still be passing through.
- Late June also works well. The Azores are never guaranteed sunshine — "four seasons in one day" is the local saying — but June gives you the best odds.

---

## What makes this trip compete with Japan or Sicily

Whale watching. The Azores are arguably the best whale watching destination in the North Atlantic — 28 cetacean species recorded, sperm whales resident year-round, dolphins on virtually every trip, and a system of clifftop vigias (former whaling lookout towers) that radio-guide boats to live sightings. Neither Japan nor Sicily offers anything remotely comparable.

Then there is Madeira's levada network: 3,000+ kilometers of irrigation channels with walking paths alongside them, running through UNESCO-listed laurel forests, along cliff edges, through hand-carved tunnels, and past waterfalls that cross the trail. The levadas are flat — they follow contour lines — which means you get dramatic mountain scenery without the knee-destroying descents. For a group with mixed fitness levels and a possibly pregnant member, this is ideal: the easy levadas are gentle enough for anyone, the hard ones are genuinely exposed, and the Pico do Arieiro to Pico Ruivo ridge hike (Madeira's highest peaks) is a world-class mountain traverse above the clouds.

Add volcanic hot springs on Sao Miguel, a golden-sand beach island (Porto Santo), the Atlantic's clearest diving waters, and overall costs well below Japan or Sicily, and this is a trip that delivers more variety per day than it has any right to.

## Section by section

### Sao Miguel, Azores (4 nights)

The largest Azorean island, and the one with the most infrastructure. Sao Miguel is a volcanic island of crater lakes, hot springs, tea plantations (Europe's only), and some of the best whale watching on earth. Four nights lets you hit every highlight without rushing.

**Sete Cidades** is the image that sells the Azores: twin crater lakes — one blue, one green — inside a massive volcanic caldera, ringed by hydrangea hedges that bloom in June. The rim hike is roughly 12 kilometers and circles the entire caldera with views that change at every turn. On a clear day it is extraordinary. On a foggy day the lakes disappear entirely — this is the Azores, and you accept the weather. Drive to the rim viewpoints (Vista do Rei, Boca do Inferno) on whichever morning has the clearest forecast.

**Furnas** is a geothermal valley in the island's interior. Steam rises from the ground. Volcanic stew — cozido das Furnas — is lowered into holes in the earth in the morning and pulled out six hours later, cooked by the planet's heat. Terra Nostra Park has a large iron-rich thermal pool (warm, orange-tinted, surrounded by botanical gardens) where you soak under old trees. Dona Beija hot springs nearby offer a more modern experience with multiple pools at varying temperatures.

**Whale watching** from Ponta Delgada is the headline activity. Half-day trips on RIBs or small motor vessels. A vigia (lookout spotter) on the cliffs radios the boat captain to guide you to live sightings. Sperm whales: year-round residents, very likely in June. Common dolphins, bottlenose dolphins, Risso's dolphins: nearly guaranteed. Sei whales, fin whales: possible in early June (migration season winding down). You may also see loggerhead sea turtles. The experience is not passive — you are on the open Atlantic, the whales are wild, the encounters are real.

**Other highlights:** Islet of Vila Franca do Campo — a volcanic crater that forms a natural swimming pool in the sea, limited daily visitors, book ahead. Gorreana Tea Plantation — Europe's only tea estate, free tours, rolling green rows against the Atlantic. Pineapple greenhouses in Ponta Delgada — the Azorean greenhouse pineapple is small, sweet, and unlike anything commercial.

### Lisbon layover (travel day)

The connection between the Azores and Madeira runs through Lisbon — there is no practical direct route. This is an honest travel day. Fly Ponta Delgada → Lisbon (~2.5 hrs), then Lisbon → Funchal (~1.5 hrs). With a connection, the full transit takes most of a day. If you can build a 4–6 hour layover, a quick Lisbon stop is pleasant (pastéis de nata at the airport will do), but don't try to sightsee — save it for another trip.

### Madeira (5 nights)

![Levada walk through Madeira's laurel forest](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Laurisilva_da_Madeira_2.jpg/1280px-Laurisilva_da_Madeira_2.jpg)
*Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Laurisilva_da_Madeira_2.jpg)*

Madeira is a volcanic island 57 kilometers long and 22 wide, rising steeply from the Atlantic to peaks above 1,800 meters. The south coast (Funchal) is warm and dry. The north coast is cloud-forested and wet. The interior is a wall of green cliffs, waterfalls, and laurel forest that has existed for millions of years — a relict of forests that once covered southern Europe, now UNESCO-listed. Five nights gives you the levada hikes, the mountain traverse, Funchal's food and market scene, a beach day on Porto Santo, and the splurge night.

**Levada walks** are the reason to come to Madeira. The system is unique: centuries-old irrigation channels carry water from the wet north to the dry south, with narrow maintenance paths alongside. You walk along the levada, following the water through forests, along cliff edges, and through tunnels (bring a headlamp). The gradient is negligible — the channels follow contour lines — so the walking is flat even when the scenery is vertical.

- **Levada das 25 Fontes** (25 Fountains): ~10km round trip, moderate. Ends at a waterfall amphitheatre with water pouring from 25 separate sources. One of Madeira's most famous walks.
- **Levada do Caldeirão Verde** (Green Cauldron): ~13km round trip, moderate. Through ancient laurel forest to a waterfall falling into a green pool inside a volcanic bowl. Tunnels along the way.
- **Ponta de São Lourenço**: The eastern peninsula — a dramatic coastal walk along exposed red-and-gold cliffs above the Atlantic. 4–5 hours round trip. Different character from the levadas — arid, windswept, panoramic.
- Easy options for Lillian if pregnant: Levada dos Balcões (short, viewpoint over a valley), Levada do Rei, any number of gentle paths.

**Pico do Arieiro to Pico Ruivo** is the serious mountain day. You drive to the summit of Arieiro (1,818m — Madeira's third highest peak, with a paved road to the top). From there, a ridge trail runs to Pico Ruivo (1,862m — the highest point). The hike is 7 kilometers one way (or 11km via the longer route), with tunnels, exposed ridgelines, and significant up-and-down. On a clear day you walk above the clouds with the Atlantic in every direction. 3–5 hours one way. Not technical, but strenuous and exposed. This is the hike for Phil, Steph, Juan, and Christian.

**Funchal** is a proper city with a proper food scene. The Mercado dos Lavradores (Workers' Market) is the centerpiece — tropical fruits you may never have seen (monstera deliciosa, tamarillo, custard apple), fish from the deep Atlantic (black scabbardfish, tuna), and flower stalls. The Old Town (Zona Velha) has the painted doors project — every door on Rua de Santa Maria decorated by a different artist — plus restaurants and bars. Bolo do caco (garlic flatbread) is addictive and available everywhere. Espetada (beef on laurel-wood skewers, hung vertically at the table) is the signature dish. Madeira wine at Blandy's Wine Lodge. Coffee is solid throughout.

**Monte Palace Tropical Garden** sits above Funchal, reached by cable car. Tile panels, Japanese garden sections, views over the city. The descent by toboggan — wicker basket sleds on runners, controlled by two men in white, sliding down the streets — has been running since 1850. Tourist-ish but uniquely Madeiran.

**Whale and dolphin watching from Funchal:** Shorter trips than the Azores but still excellent. Short-finned pilot whales are nearly guaranteed (resident population). Bottlenose and common dolphins on most trips. Occasional sperm whales. Deep water starts very close to shore, which is why the encounters happen quickly.

### Porto Santo (day trip from Madeira)

Madeira has pebble and rock beaches. Porto Santo, 43 kilometers northeast, has a 9-kilometer golden sand beach — the real thing. The ferry takes 2.5 hours each way (or a 15-minute flight). This is a beach day: swim, walk the sand, eat grilled fish at a beachfront restaurant, take the ferry home. Simple and exactly right after days of mountain hiking. The sand is supposedly therapeutic — high calcium carbonate content. Believe what you want; the beach is beautiful regardless.

## Getting there and back

**SFO → Azores (Ponta Delgada, PDL):** No direct flights from the West Coast. Best routing: SFO → Boston/JFK → Ponta Delgada (Azores Airlines/SATA operates seasonal direct BOS→PDL, ~5 hours). Or: SFO → Lisbon (nonstop, ~10.5 hrs on TAP or United) → Ponta Delgada (~2.5 hrs on TAP or Azores Airlines). Total: ~15–18 hours with layover. The Boston routing is faster if schedules align.

**Azores → Madeira:** Fly Ponta Delgada → Lisbon (~2.5 hrs) → Funchal (~1.5 hrs). No practical direct connection. Full travel day.

**Madeira → SFO:** Fly Funchal → Lisbon (~1.5 hrs) → SFO (nonstop, ~10.5 hrs on TAP or United). Or connect through any European hub.

**Internal transport:**
- **Sao Miguel:** Rent 2 cars at Ponta Delgada Airport. Essential — public transport is sparse. The island is ~65km long; everything is within an hour's drive. Roads are good.
- **Madeira:** Rent 2 cars at Funchal Airport. Roads are steep, winding, and sometimes narrow. Tunnels are common and well-lit. The via rápida highway along the south coast is modern. Mountain roads require comfort with switchbacks.
- **Porto Santo:** Ferry from Funchal (Portosanto Line, 2.5 hrs) or 15-minute flight (limited seats, book ahead for 6).

## Day-by-day

### Day 1: Arrive Sao Miguel
Fly SFO → Lisbon (or Boston) → Ponta Delgada. Arrive afternoon or evening. Pick up rental cars at the airport. Check in. Easy first dinner in Ponta Delgada — grilled fish, lapas (limpets with garlic butter), Azorean bread. Walk the waterfront.

### Day 2: Whale watching + Furnas
Morning: **Whale watching half-day trip** from Ponta Delgada. Depart ~8:30am, return ~12:30pm. Sperm whales and dolphins are the targets.
Afternoon: Drive to Furnas (~45 min). Visit the fumaroles. Pick up a pot of cozido das Furnas from the underground cooking holes (arrange through your hotel or a local guide — or eat at Tony's, the famous cozido restaurant).
Late afternoon: **Terra Nostra Park** — soak in the thermal pool. The iron-rich orange water stains swimsuits (bring an old one).
Evening: Dinner in Furnas or drive back to Ponta Delgada.

*Note for Lillian if pregnant:* Terra Nostra's main pool is ~35–40°C. Too hot. Dona Beija nearby has cooler pools — check temperatures. Or skip soaking and walk the botanical gardens instead.

### Day 3: Sete Cidades
Morning: Check the weather. If clear, drive to Sete Cidades immediately.
**Rim hike** (~12km, moderate, 4–5 hours) for Phil, Steph, Juan, Christian. The full circuit with views into both lakes.
**Viewpoint circuit** for Eliza and Lillian: Drive to Vista do Rei and Boca do Inferno viewpoints. Walk down to the lakeside village for lunch. Easy and beautiful.
Afternoon: Return to Ponta Delgada. Gorreana Tea Plantation (free tour, tea tasting, green fields against the Atlantic).
Evening: Best group dinner of the Azores stay.

### Day 4: East coast + Vila Franca
Morning: Islet of Vila Franca do Campo — the volcanic crater swimming pool in the sea. Book the limited daily entry in advance. Swim, snorkel, sunbathe on volcanic rock.
Afternoon: Faial da Terra waterfall hike (Salto do Prego, ~2 hours round trip, moderate, through lush valley to a waterfall).
Or: Surfing at Santa Barbara beach on the north coast (Phil — rental boards available).
Evening: Pineapple plantation visit in Ponta Delgada. Final Azorean dinner.

### Day 5: Travel day — Azores to Madeira
Morning: Fly Ponta Delgada → Lisbon (~2.5 hrs).
Afternoon: Fly Lisbon → Funchal (~1.5 hrs).
Pick up rental cars at Funchal Airport. Check in.
Evening: Walk Funchal's Old Town. Bolo do caco and poncha (for those who drink). Quiet night.

### Day 6: Levada das 25 Fontes + Caldeirão Verde
Full hiking day. The two most famous levada walks start from the same area (Rabaçal, in the western mountains, ~1 hr drive from Funchal):
**25 Fontes** (~10km, 3–4 hrs) for everyone — moderate, through laurel forest to the waterfall amphitheatre. Flat levada walking with one descent/ascent section.
**Caldeirão Verde** (~13km, 4–5 hrs) for the strong hikers — continue deeper into the forest, through tunnels, to the green cauldron waterfall.
Lillian and Eliza can do 25 Fontes and turn back, or split off for the shorter Levada do Risco (25-minute walk to a single dramatic waterfall). Everyone gets a great hike.
Evening: Dinner in Funchal. Espetada (the vertical beef skewers) at a traditional restaurant.

### Day 7: Pico do Arieiro → Pico Ruivo
**Summit group** (Phil, Christian, Juan, Steph): Drive to Pico do Arieiro before dawn (1 hr from Funchal). Hike the ridge to Pico Ruivo (7km, 3–5 hrs one way). Above the clouds. Tunnels. Exposed ridgeline. One of the best day hikes in Europe. Arrange return transport (taxi from Achada do Teixeira trailhead on the Ruivo side, or out-and-back from Arieiro).
**Easier day** (Eliza, Lillian): Mercado dos Lavradores in the morning (the market is best before 11am). Monte Palace Tropical Garden by cable car. Optional: toboggan ride down from Monte. Afternoon: Funchal Old Town shopping, painted doors walk, coffee.
Evening: Regroup. Group dinner.

### Day 8: Ponta de Sao Lourenco + splurge check-in
Morning: Drive to Madeira's eastern tip. **Ponta de Sao Lourenco hike** (~4–5 hrs round trip, moderate). Dramatic coastal cliffs, arid landscape, completely different from the lush levadas. Everyone can do this one — it is exposed but not strenuous.
Afternoon: Check into the splurge hotel — **Belmond Reid's Palace.** Clifftop above the Atlantic, opened in 1891, afternoon tea on the terrace (Churchill was a regular). Rooms from ~€400–800/night depending on category. Pool, spa, gardens, exceptional restaurant.
Evening: Dinner at the hotel or one of Funchal's best restaurants. This is the night.

### Day 9: Porto Santo beach day
Morning: Ferry from Funchal to Porto Santo (~2.5 hrs). Or the 15-minute flight if seats are available.
Full day: 9 kilometers of golden sand beach. Swim, walk, read, eat grilled fish at a beachside restaurant. This is the decompression day after a week of hiking and volcanoes. Eliza's beach day. Phil can kitesurf if conditions allow (Porto Santo has consistent wind).
Evening: Ferry back to Funchal. Light dinner.

### Day 10: Whale watching Madeira + free afternoon
Morning: **Dolphin and whale watching trip** from Funchal (~3 hrs). Pilot whales nearly guaranteed. Dolphins on most trips.
Afternoon: Free. Last shopping in Funchal. Blandy's Wine Lodge for a Madeira wine tasting. Return to a favorite levada for a second walk. Or simply rest.
Evening: Final group dinner. Poncha for the drinkers. Early-ish night.

### Day 11: North coast drive + departure prep
Morning: Drive Madeira's north coast road — dramatic cliffs, waterfalls falling directly into the sea, tiny villages. São Vicente lava tubes (volcanic caves, guided tour, ~30 min). Stop at a north-coast restaurant for fresh fish.
Afternoon: Return to Funchal. Pack. Drop cars if departing early tomorrow.
Evening: Last dinner. Or cook at the Airbnb if accommodations allow.

### Day 12: Fly home
Morning: Fly Funchal → Lisbon → SFO. Or connect through another European hub.
Allow ample time for the Lisbon connection.

## What to book before you leave

In approximate order of urgency:
1. **Belmond Reid's Palace, Funchal** — 3 rooms, 1–2 nights. Iconic property, books ahead for June.
2. **Azores whale watching** — half-day trip from Ponta Delgada, 6 people. Futurismo or Terra Azul are well-regarded operators. Book weeks ahead for June.
3. **Vila Franca do Campo islet** — limited daily visitors, reserve in advance (Câmara Municipal de Vila Franca manages access).
4. **Rental cars** — 2 cars on Sao Miguel (pick up/drop off PDL airport), 2 cars on Madeira (pick up/drop off FNC airport). Book early — island fleets are small.
5. **Flights** — SFO → Ponta Delgada, Ponta Delgada → Lisbon → Funchal, Funchal → Lisbon → SFO. TAP and Azores Airlines. Book the full routing together if possible, or as separate legs. 6 seats on the small inter-island planes requires early booking.
6. **Porto Santo ferry** — Portosanto Line (Lobo Marinho ferry). Check summer schedule, book for 6.
7. **Madeira whale watching** — half-day from Funchal, 6 people. VMT Madeira or Rota dos Cetáceos.
8. **Furnas cozido experience** — if you want the traditional underground cooking, arrange through Terra Nostra Garden Hotel or a local guide.
9. **Pico do Arieiro logistics** — no permit required, but arrange return transport (taxi from Achada do Teixeira) for point-to-point hikers.
10. **Funchal dinner reservations** — book 1–2 strong restaurants for the group.

## Key risks

**Azores fog is the single biggest variable.** Sete Cidades is famous for being socked in. The rim hike and viewpoints require clear weather. Mitigation: monitor the forecast daily and move the Sete Cidades day to whichever morning looks clearest. With 4 nights on Sao Miguel, you have flexibility. Accept that the Azores are a moody destination — the fog is part of the character, and some days will be grey.

**The Azores-to-Madeira transit is a full day.** There is no direct flight between the archipelagos. You fly through Lisbon. This costs a day. It is the price of combining both destinations. The alternative — doing only one — means missing either the whale watching (Azores) or the levadas (Madeira). The combination is worth the transit.

**Funchal airport crosswinds.** Madeira's airport has a famously short runway extended on stilts over the sea. Crosswinds occasionally divert flights to Porto Santo or Lisbon. More common in winter, but it can happen. Don't schedule anything time-critical on arrival/departure days.

**Atlantic sea conditions.** Whale watching trips, the Porto Santo ferry, and any boat excursions can be cancelled due to swells. June is the calmest season, but the Atlantic is never guaranteed flat. Build flexibility — if whale watching is cancelled on Day 2, try again on Day 4.

**Lillian / pregnancy:** Hot springs are the main concern — Terra Nostra's pool runs ~35–40°C, which is too hot in early pregnancy. Dona Beija has cooler options. All levada walks at easy/moderate difficulty are fine. Pico do Arieiro → Pico Ruivo is not recommended (strenuous, exposed). Whale watching boats in calm conditions are fine; in rough seas, the bouncing could aggravate nausea. Porto Santo beach day is perfect for her.

**Vegetarian eating is manageable but requires planning.** Portuguese island cuisine is meat-and-fish-heavy. In Funchal and Ponta Delgada, modern restaurants offer vegetarian options, and there are a few dedicated vegetarian/vegan spots. Traditional restaurants in smaller towns will be limited — soups may have meat stock, and "vegetarian" sometimes still includes fish. Self-catering from markets helps. Bread, cheese, eggs, salads, and pasta are universally available. Not as easy as Sicily's deep vegetable tradition, but far better than rural Japan.

**Tattoos are a non-issue.** Portugal has no cultural restrictions around tattoos at beaches, pools, hot springs, or public spaces.

**Shopping is limited.** This is not a shopping destination. Funchal has the market, embroidery shops, and wine. Ponta Delgada has tea and pineapple products. Neither has anything approaching the shopping options of Tokyo, Palermo, or even Taormina. Manage Eliza's expectations. The trade-off is everything else.
