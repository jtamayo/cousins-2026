# Trip D: Volcanoes, Ruins, and the Sea
### Sicily — 11 days

![Taormina's ancient Greek theatre with Mt. Etna smoking in the background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Taormina_BW_2012-10-05_16-09-43.jpg/1280px-Taormina_BW_2012-10-05_16-09-43.jpg)
*Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Taormina_BW_2012-10-05_16-09-43.jpg)*

On your third night you are eating chickpea fritters from a market stall in a Palermo alley while a man on a Vespa squeezes past your elbow. Two days later you are watching Stromboli erupt against the night sky from a volcanic island in the Tyrrhenian Sea. A week in, you are standing inside a 2,500-year-old Greek temple that is better preserved than anything in Athens. Sicily is not Italy — it is Sicily, and it delivers something no Japanese itinerary can: guaranteed Mediterranean sun, volcanic islands you can swim around, and the deepest food culture in southern Europe, half of which happens to be vegetarian.

---

## At a glance

- **11 days / 10 nights**
- Palermo (2N) → Aeolian Islands (3N, base on Lipari) → Taormina (2N) → Syracuse / Ortigia (2N) → Val di Noto day trip → fly home from Catania
- Fly into Palermo (PMO), out of Catania (CTA) — no backtracking
- 2 rental cars for the mainland portions; ferries between Milazzo and the Aeolian Islands
- **Best timing: Early-to-mid June** (June 1–20). Warm but not scorching (25–30°C), almost no rain, sea temperature 21–23°C and rising. Late June is slightly hotter and busier but still excellent. June is widely considered the single best month for Sicily.

---

## What makes this trip compete with Japan

The Japan trips offer cultural novelty and nature that feels like another planet. Sicily offers something different: a place where you swim in turquoise water after hiking an active volcano, eat street food in markets that have operated for a thousand years, and walk through Greek temples older than the Parthenon — all in guaranteed sunshine. The Aeolian Islands are a volcanic archipelago with snorkeling visibility of 20–30 meters, a volcano that erupts every 15 minutes, and sulfur mud baths at the edge of a crater. The food is extraordinary and half of it — arancini, caponata, pasta alla Norma, panelle, granita — is naturally vegetarian. Tattoos are a complete non-issue. The pregnancy logistics are far simpler. And the weather is the most reliable of any option on the table.

## Section by section

### Palermo (2 nights)

![Palermo's Ballarò street market](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Palermo-Market-bjs-1.jpg/1280px-Palermo-Market-bjs-1.jpg)
*Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Palermo-Market-bjs-1.jpg)*

Palermo is loud, chaotic, beautiful, and it has the best street food in Europe. Three historic markets — Ballarò, Vucciria, Capo — run through the old city like arteries, selling fried chickpea fritters (panelle), rice balls the size of softballs (arancini), grilled octopus, and cannoli filled to order. You eat standing up, surrounded by shouting vendors and stacked crates of blood oranges. This is the welcome meal, and it sets the tone for the whole trip.

Beyond the markets: the Cappella Palatina inside the Norman Palace has gold Byzantine mosaics that are among the finest medieval artworks in existence. Monreale Cathedral, 15 minutes uphill from the city, has 6,340 square meters of gold mosaic covering every surface. The Kalsa neighborhood has galleries, craft workshops, and a growing vintage scene. Kappabashi this is not — but the old-town kitchen supply shops carry moka pots, pasta tools, and artisan knives that are worth the suitcase space.

### Aeolian Islands (3 nights, base on Lipari)

Seven volcanic islands scattered across the Tyrrhenian Sea north of Sicily. You take a hydrofoil from Milazzo (1–1.5 hours) and arrive somewhere that feels more like a Greek island chain than anything Italian. The water is clear, the villages are white, and three of the islands have active volcanic features you can walk on.

**Lipari** is the largest island and the best base: a proper town with restaurants, a good archaeological museum, pumice beaches where the sand is white volcanic powder, and boat tour operators who run day circuits to the surrounding islands.

**Vulcano** is a 10-minute boat ride from Lipari. The Gran Cratere hike takes an hour to climb — sulfur vents hissing around you, views across the entire archipelago from the rim. At the base, free sulfur mud baths sit in a natural depression near the port. The water offshore bubbles with underwater fumaroles — you can snorkel over warm volcanic vents. It smells like hell and feels like a spa.

**Stromboli** is the reason this itinerary exists for the adventure-inclined. The volcano erupts roughly every 15–20 minutes — small explosions that throw incandescent rock into the air. The guided night hike to the 400-meter viewpoint (3 hours round trip, moderate) puts you close enough to watch eruptions against the dark sky. The full summit (924m) has been restricted since 2019, but the 400m viewpoint is spectacular. This is a day trip or overnight from Lipari.

**Panarea** is tiny, glamorous, and has Cala Junco — a volcanic cove with turquoise water and excellent snorkeling. A half-day boat stop.

**Salina** is the green island: fern forests, capers, Malvasia dessert wine, and Monte Fossa delle Felci (962m, 3–4 hours, a serious hike through lush vegetation to the highest point in the Aeolians).

Snorkeling across the islands runs 20–30 meters of visibility. You see grouper, barracuda, moray eels, octopus, and volcanic rock formations underwater. Not tropical reef — better described as dramatic Mediterranean with volcanic geology.

### Taormina (2 nights — SPLURGE)

![View from Taormina's Greek Theatre toward the coast and Etna](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Taormina-Greek_Theatre.jpg/1280px-Taormina-Greek_Theatre.jpg)
*Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Taormina-Greek_Theatre.jpg)*

Taormina is built on a cliff 200 meters above the Ionian Sea, with Etna filling the western horizon. The ancient Greek Theatre — 3rd century BC, still used for performances in June — frames the volcano in its broken arch. Corso Umberto is the pedestrian main street: ceramics shops, gelato, linen boutiques, aperitivo bars. Isola Bella, the beach below town, is pebbly and beautiful with good snorkeling. This is the splurge stop.

**The splurge hotel:** The Four Seasons San Domenico Palace — a 15th-century Dominican monastery converted to a hotel, reopened after a full renovation in 2021 (featured in Season 2 of The White Lotus). Cliff-edge terraces, infinity pool, Etna views. Or the Belmond Grand Hotel Timeo, directly beside the Greek Theatre, with gardens and a Michelin-quality restaurant. Either property in June runs roughly €700–1,200/night. For one or two nights, this is the right use of the splurge budget.

### Mt. Etna (day trip from Taormina)

Europe's tallest and most active volcano, 3,357 meters. The south side approach starts at Rifugio Sapienza (1,900m) — cable car to 2,500m, then guided 4x4 and hike to the ~2,900m crater zone. The standard tourist version takes 4–5 hours.

**For Phil, Steph, Juan, and Christian:** A full summit attempt with an authorized guide reaches the active craters at ~3,300m. An 8–10 hour day on loose volcanic terrain at altitude. Genuinely strenuous and genuinely unforgettable.

**For Eliza and Lillian:** Cable car to 2,500m, short walk around the Silvestri Craters at the Rifugio level, views in every direction, back in town for lunch. Or skip Etna entirely and spend the morning at Isola Bella beach and the afternoon shopping Corso Umberto.

**Etna wine country:** The volcanic soil on Etna's lower slopes produces world-class wine (Nerello Mascalese reds, Carricante whites). Winery visits at Benanti, Planeta, or Tenuta delle Terre Nere. An afternoon of wine tasting on a volcano is a strong alternative for a non-summit day.

### Syracuse and Ortigia (2 nights)

Ortigia is the historic island center of Syracuse — a tiny island connected by a short bridge, with baroque piazzas, a cathedral built directly into the columns of a 5th-century-BC Greek temple (you can see the Doric columns embedded in the walls), a morning market with swordfish and blood oranges, and waterfront restaurants where you eat fresh seafood as the sun sets over the harbor.

The Neapolis Archaeological Park on the mainland side has a Greek theatre from the 5th century BC — still used for performances in June — the Ear of Dionysius (a cave with extraordinary acoustics), and a Roman amphitheatre. This is where Greek civilization collided with Roman power, and the ruins are some of the best-preserved outside Greece.

The evening passeggiata on Ortigia — families, gelato, the slow parade — is one of the most atmospheric walks in Sicily.

### Val di Noto (day trip from Syracuse)

After a catastrophic earthquake in 1693 destroyed dozens of towns in southeastern Sicily, they were rebuilt from scratch in a unified late-Baroque style. Three of these towns are UNESCO-listed and make an extraordinary day trip from Syracuse:

**Noto:** Honey-colored limestone facades, a single grand corso lined with churches and palaces, and Caffè Sicilia — one of the most famous pastry shops in Italy, known for its granita and cassata.

**Modica:** Built into a ravine, famous for ancient Aztec-style chocolate — grainy, unrefined, made with a pre-Columbian technique brought by the Spanish. Chocolate shops on every street.

**Ragusa Ibla:** A hilltop old town with fewer tourists and dramatic views. The three towns are within an hour of each other and an hour from Syracuse.

## Getting there and back

**SFO → Sicily:** No direct flights. Best routing: SFO → Rome FCO (nonstop, ~11.5 hrs on United, Delta, or ITA Airways) → Palermo PMO (~1 hr domestic). Total: ~14–16 hours with layover. Alternative: connect through London, Paris, or Frankfurt. **Fly into Palermo, out of Catania** — open-jaw ticket avoids backtracking.

**Internal transport:** Rent 2 cars at Palermo Airport on arrival. Drive Palermo → Cefalù (optional stop, 1 hr) → Milazzo (2.5 hrs from Palermo). Park cars at Milazzo, take the hydrofoil to Lipari (~1–1.5 hrs). Return to Milazzo after the Aeolians, drive to Taormina (~1.5 hrs), then Syracuse (~1.5 hrs). Drop cars at Catania Airport.

**Aeolian ferries:** Liberty Lines and Siremar operate hydrofoils from Milazzo to Lipari (frequent, ~1 hr) and between the islands. Stromboli is ~2.5 hrs from Lipari by hydrofoil or ~1.5 hrs from Milazzo. Book in advance for a group of 6.

**Departure:** Fly Catania CTA → Rome FCO (~1 hr) → SFO nonstop. Or connect through any major European hub.

## Day-by-day

### Day 1: Arrive Palermo
Fly SFO → Rome → Palermo. Arrive evening. Check in. Easy first dinner near the hotel — a plate of pasta alla Norma and a glass of Nero d'Avola. Walk the streets. Adjust.

### Day 2: Palermo — markets and mosaics
Morning: Street food walking tour through Ballarò and Capo markets. Panelle, arancini, sfincione. Juan: kitchen supply shops in the old town.
Midday: Cappella Palatina (the gold mosaics).
Afternoon: Drive or taxi to Monreale Cathedral (15 min). Then back to Palermo — Kalsa neighborhood for Steph's vintage shopping, Eliza's boutiques.
Evening: Group dinner at a proper Palermo restaurant. Book ahead.

### Day 3: Drive to Milazzo, ferry to Lipari
Morning: Pick up rental cars. Drive to Milazzo (~2.5 hrs, with optional 30-minute stop in Cefalù for the cathedral and a quick beach walk).
Park cars at the Milazzo port lot. Afternoon hydrofoil to Lipari (~1 hr).
Check in. Walk the town. First Aeolian dinner — grilled swordfish, caponata, cold local wine.

### Day 4: Vulcano + Stromboli night hike
Morning: Boat to Vulcano (10 min). Gran Cratere hike (1 hr up, everyone can do this). Sulfur mud baths at the port (skip if Lillian is pregnant — sulfur exposure). Snorkel the underwater fumaroles.
Afternoon: Return to Lipari. Rest.
Evening: **Stromboli night hike.** Take the late afternoon hydrofoil to Stromboli. Guided hike to the 400m viewpoint. Watch the eruptions in the dark. Return to Lipari by late boat (or overnight on Stromboli). This is the day everyone remembers.

*Alternative for Lillian if pregnant:* Skip the Vulcano mud baths (sulfur) and the Stromboli night hike (strenuous + volcanic gas). Spend the afternoon on Lipari's beaches and the evening at a restaurant with a view. Entirely pleasant.

### Day 5: Island hopping — Panarea and Salina
Full-day boat tour (hire a private boat for 6, or join a group tour):
Morning: Panarea — swim at Cala Junco, snorkel the volcanic cove.
Afternoon: Salina — lunch in Santa Marina Salina. Malvasia wine tasting. Optional: Monte Fossa delle Felci hike (3–4 hrs, serious — Phil and whoever is up for it).
Return to Lipari for final Aeolian dinner.

### Day 6: Ferry back, drive to Taormina
Morning hydrofoil to Milazzo. Collect cars. Drive to Taormina (~1.5 hrs).
Check into the splurge hotel (San Domenico Palace or Grand Hotel Timeo).
Afternoon: Greek Theatre. Walk Corso Umberto. Isola Bella beach if time.
Evening: Aperitivo on the terrace with Etna in the background. Dinner at the hotel or a Taormina restaurant.

### Day 7: Mt. Etna
**Summit group** (Phil, Christian, Juan, Steph): Depart early. Drive to Rifugio Sapienza (~1 hr). Cable car + guided summit attempt. 8–10 hours. Return exhausted and elated.
**Easier group** (Eliza, Lillian): Cable car to 2,500m, walk the Silvestri Craters, back to Taormina by lunch. Afternoon: Etna wine country — winery visit and tasting at Tenuta delle Terre Nere or Benanti. Or: Isola Bella beach and Taormina shopping.
Evening: Regroup. Strong group dinner in Taormina.

### Day 8: Drive to Syracuse
Morning: Final Taormina morning. One last look at Etna from the theatre.
Drive to Syracuse (~1.5 hrs). Check in (Ortigia old town hotel or Airbnb).
Afternoon: Walk Ortigia. The cathedral with its embedded Greek columns. Ortigia morning market (afternoon remnants). Fonte Aretusa.
Evening: Seafood dinner on the Ortigia waterfront.

### Day 9: Syracuse + Val di Noto
Morning: Neapolis Archaeological Park — Greek theatre, Ear of Dionysius, Roman amphitheatre. 2–3 hours.
Afternoon: Drive to Noto (~45 min). Walk the corso. Granita at Caffè Sicilia. Continue to Modica for chocolate. Ragusa Ibla if time allows.
Return to Syracuse for a final dinner on Ortigia.

### Day 10: Free morning, drive to Catania area
Morning: Free in Syracuse. Swim at a nearby beach, or return to Ortigia for last-minute shopping and coffee.
Afternoon: Drive to Catania area (~1 hr). Check in near the airport or explore Catania itself — the fish market (La Pescheria) is the most theatrical in Sicily. Via Etnea for a final passeggiata.
Evening: Final dinner in Catania.

### Day 11: Fly home
Morning: Drop cars at Catania Airport. Fly CTA → Rome → SFO.

## What to book before you leave

In approximate order of urgency:
1. **Splurge hotel in Taormina** — San Domenico Palace (Four Seasons) or Belmond Grand Hotel Timeo. 3 rooms, 2 nights. These fill months ahead for June.
2. **Aeolian Islands accommodation** — 3 rooms on Lipari (hotel or Airbnb), 3 nights. Good properties book early.
3. **Stromboli night hike guide** — certified volcanic guide, 6 people, book well ahead. Magmatrek and Stromboli Adventures are established operators.
4. **Aeolian hydrofoil tickets** — Liberty Lines, Milazzo ↔ Lipari and inter-island. Book online when schedules release.
5. **Rental cars** — 2 mid-size cars, pick up Palermo PMO, drop off Catania CTA. Book early for one-way drop-off.
6. **International Driving Permits** — from AAA before departure (technically required in Italy).
7. **Etna summit guide** — authorized guide for the full summit attempt. Book 2–4 weeks ahead.
8. **Palermo group dinner** — one strong restaurant, booked for 6.
9. **Flights** — open-jaw (SFO → Rome → Palermo PMO // Catania CTA → Rome → SFO). Book as a group.
10. **Ortigia accommodation** — 3 rooms or a house rental on the island, 2 nights.

## Key risks

**Stromboli access can be restricted at any time.** Volcanic activity determines access levels. Since the 2019 major eruption, the summit has been off-limits — only guided hikes to the 400m Sciara del Fuoco viewpoint are permitted. Even this can close if activity increases. Build it into Day 4 and treat it as the plan, but accept that it could be cancelled. If so, the day becomes a longer Vulcano + Panarea circuit instead.

**Etna summit access depends on volcanic activity.** The INGV (Italian volcanology institute) sets alert levels. Lower-elevation excursions (cable car, Silvestri Craters) are almost always accessible. The full summit may not be. Check 2 weeks before departure.

**Sicilian driving is aggressive.** The highways are fine. The secondary roads are narrow and winding. Historic center ZTL zones (limited traffic zones) issue automatic fines if you drive in without a permit — use GPS, park outside old towns, and walk in. Allow extra time for everything.

**Aeolian ferry cancellations.** Rough seas can ground hydrofoils, though this is uncommon in June. The 3-night Lipari stay provides buffer. If one day is cancelled, you reorganize the island-hopping circuit.

**Lillian / pregnancy:** No diving, no Vulcano sulfur mud baths (sulfur + heat exposure), Stromboli night hike not recommended (exertion + volcanic gas), full Etna summit not recommended. Everything else — snorkeling, easy hikes, boat tours, Vulcano crater rim (just skip the mud), all the food and towns — is fine. Sicily's lower intensity and warm-weather walking make pregnancy management significantly easier than the Japan options.

**Tattoos are a complete non-issue.** No restrictions at any beach, pool, hotel, hot spring, or public space. This is a genuine logistical advantage over every Japan itinerary.

**Vegetarian eating is excellent.** Sicily has one of the deepest vegetable-first food traditions in the Mediterranean: pasta alla Norma, caponata, arancini al burro, panelle, parmigiana, busiate al pesto trapanese, all the pastries. Steph will eat better here than on any other trip option. Fish broth can appear in some dishes without warning — ask "senza pesce, senza carne" to be sure.

**Heat in late June.** Early June (25–28°C) is ideal. Late June can push 30–33°C, especially inland and on Etna's lower slopes. Plan strenuous activities for morning. Stay hydrated. The coast and islands have sea breezes.
